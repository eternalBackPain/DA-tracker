SPATIAL DEVELOPMENT APPLICATION TRACKER

The premise: Development Applications, aggregated by density, mapped across Australia.

A gauge of gentrification, or notice of nuisance – this tracker plots DAs in a 3D space to inform you of any recent changes to land use around a given location.

Local councils fail to provide this data intuitively. Their websites do not capture other locailities, often do not provide address specific data, and do not visualise the density of applications in an area.

How does the tracker work?

This app uses google maps and deck.gl to plot and visualise data in 3D. It uses Vite as a build tool and Express for a backend server that calls to the PlanningAlerts API. PlanningAlerts is attributed to the OpenAustralia Foundation. The PlanningAlerts service is intended to help people be aware of what's happening in their local neighborhood, and to enable a civil discussion about those changes. Essentially, the app plots development applications on a map, clusters the results by density, and displays the results as 3D pillars.


What is the process of Development Applications in Australia?

The Environmental Planning and Assessment Act 1979 (NSW) (EP&A) regulates any development in NSW. Generally, DAs require the assessment of environmental impacts to inform whether an application should be approved or rejected.

 

The EP&A uses Environmental Planning Instruments (EPIs) to regulate land use and set out what can and cannot be developed. EPIs comprise of State Environmental Planning Policies (SEPPs) and Local Environmental Plans (LEPs).

  • LEPs allocate various zones to parcels of land, with each zone carrying different development controls. Generally, the consent authority under each LEP is the local council, and LEPs must follow a template prescribed by the Standard Instrument.
  • SEPPs concern planning issues within the state, granting decision making power to the Minister. SEPPs override LEPs as they concern matters of state significance. Importantly, they may create additional restrictions on developments, or allow developments despite prohibitions from LEPs.

 

The relevant EPIs for your land will determine whether development will require consent, or whether the proposed development is prohibited. You can refer to zoning tables in the LEP to determine whether consent is required. In order to obtain development consent, the person proposing the development must lodge a development application with the consent authority (usually the local council). The category of development as classified by the relevant EPIs will determine the process requirements for the development. These categories are canvassed on the Planning NSW website here.

 

Upon receipt of the DA, the consent authority must decide whether to accept or reject the application with reference to section 4.15. Section 4.15 of the EP&A is paramount in determining a DA. It provides that the consent authority must consider (where relevant to the DA):

  1. any EPI or proposed EPI that applies to the land to which the DA relates;
  2. the likely impacts of the development, including environmental, social and economic impacts;
  3. the suitability of the site;
  4. any public submissions; and
  5. the public interest.

This is a jurisdictional prerequisite to deciding. The consent authority must then consider the application on its merits and make a final determination. This determination is appealable by the public or objectors in certain circumstances. To oversimplify the process: EPIs set the parameters of decision-making that section 4.15 requires.
